In the rapidly evolving landscape of smart home security, privacy concerns frequently emerge as new technologies are introduced. Ring, a prominent player in the market, finds itself at the center of such a debate with a recent class-action lawsuit challenging its facial recognition capabilities. This legal action, combined with prior regulatory scrutiny and the ongoing rollout of advanced AI features, underscores the complex privacy implications for both current owners and prospective buyers of Ring devices.

### The 2026 Sigwalt v. Amazon Class Action

In June 2026, a federal class-action lawsuit, identified as `Sigwalt v. Amazon`, was filed in Seattle. This lawsuit specifically targets Ring's "[Familiar Faces](https://quvii.com/blog/google-nest-cam-familiar-faces-june-2026-update)" feature, which was launched in late 2025. The core allegation of the lawsuit is that Ring cameras, equipped with this feature, scan and store biometric "face prints" of individuals who enter the camera's frame without their explicit knowledge or consent. This includes a wide range of individuals, from regular delivery drivers to casual visitors and neighbors. The plaintiffs contend that this practice violates privacy rights by collecting sensitive biometric data without adequate disclosure or permission.

### The 'Familiar Faces' Feature Launch (Dec 2025)

Ring officially introduced its "[Familiar Faces](https://quvii.com/blog/google-nest-cam-familiar-faces-june-2026-update)" feature to the public in December 2025. Positioned as a premium AI perk, this functionality allows users to tag up to 50 frequent visitors, enabling personalized alerts and more context-rich notifications. For instance, a user could receive an alert stating "Mom is at the front door" rather than a generic "motion detected." The feature is subscription-only, requiring users to subscribe to a higher-tier plan to access its capabilities. While marketed as a convenience, its underlying technology, which involves capturing and analyzing facial characteristics, is precisely what has drawn legal challenge and privacy scrutiny.

### Legacy Privacy Issues: The FTC Settlement Recap

The 2026 lawsuit is not Ring's first encounter with privacy-related legal troubles. It follows a legacy of privacy issues, most notably a significant settlement with the Federal Trade Commission (FTC) in 2023 and 2024. This settlement, amounting to $5.8 million, addressed allegations of employee spying and unauthorized access to customer video recordings. The FTC found that Ring employees had, in some instances, accessed private customer videos without legitimate cause, and that the company had failed to implement adequate security measures to prevent such breaches. This prior settlement sets a precedent, illustrating a pattern of privacy concerns associated with Ring's operations and data handling practices.

### State-by-State Feature Availability

The availability of Ring's "[Familiar Faces](https://quvii.com/blog/google-nest-cam-familiar-faces-june-2026-update)" feature is not uniform across the United States. Due to strict biometric privacy laws, notably Illinois's Biometric Information Privacy Act (BIPA) and similar legislation in Texas and Portland, Oregon, the "Familiar Faces" feature is currently disabled in these regions. This means that if you reside in Illinois, Texas, or Portland, OR, you will not be able to utilize this specific facial recognition functionality, regardless of your subscription tier. Buyers in these areas should understand that purchasing a Ring camera for this feature would be futile.

### The Hidden Cost of AI Subscriptions

Facial recognition and other advanced AI features are not included with the basic Ring Protect plans. Instead, they are gated behind the "Ring Home Premium" tier or the "Pro Intelligence" add-on. This significantly increases the total cost of ownership for users who wish to leverage these intelligent functionalities. While a basic Ring Protect plan might be around $5-10 per month, the "Home Premium" tier could push monthly costs into the $20-30 range, or higher with additional add-ons, turning what appears to be an affordable camera into a much larger long-term investment. This "feature paywall" is a common strategy in the smart camera industry, where core hardware capabilities are unlocked only through ongoing subscriptions.

### Cloud vs. Local AI Processing

A critical distinction in security camera technology is where the artificial intelligence processing occurs. Unlike some brands that process AI locally on the device (known as "edge AI"), Ring's facial recognition feature relies on cloud processing. This means that the biometric data, specifically the "face prints" captured by your camera, are uploaded to and stored on Amazon's servers for analysis. This approach raises concerns about data sovereignty and security, as your sensitive biometric information resides in a third-party cloud environment, potentially subject to different privacy policies, data breaches, or government requests than data processed and stored exclusively on your local device.

## Impact on existing owners

![Impact on existing owners](/img/ring-facial-recognition-lawsuit-impact-2026/inline-3.webp)


Existing Ring camera owners are directly affected by the ongoing legal challenges and policy changes surrounding facial recognition. These impacts range from new mandatory consent prompts to data retention policies and the potential for feature alterations based on legal outcomes.

### Firmware Updates and Mandatory Consent

Recent 2026 firmware updates for Ring devices have introduced mandatory "Pro Feature" consent screens within the Ring App. Users must now explicitly accept these new terms to continue utilizing advanced AI functionalities, including "Familiar Faces" and "Active Warnings." These prompts are designed to ensure users are aware of and agree to the data collection practices associated with these features. Failure to accept these terms may result in the disabling of these advanced AI capabilities on your device, even if you are subscribed to a plan that includes them. This shift represents a move towards greater transparency, albeit one driven by legal and regulatory pressures.

### How Ring Handles Your Biometric Data

Ring's official policy states that it handles biometric data with specific retention periods. The company claims to delete "unrecognized" face data after 30 days and "recognized" data (faces you have tagged as "Familiar Faces") after 180 days. However, the ongoing `Sigwalt v. Amazon` lawsuit disputes these claims, alleging that Ring's actual data retention and usage practices may differ or are not adequately transparent. The lawsuit seeks to clarify and potentially alter these policies, emphasizing the sensitive nature of biometric information and the need for robust protections.

### Will Your Camera Lose Features?

The outcome of the `Sigwalt v. Amazon` lawsuit could have significant implications for the availability of Ring's facial recognition features. If the lawsuit succeeds, Ring may be compelled to disable facial recognition nationwide, or at the very least, implement more stringent "opt-in" requirements, such as requiring property owners to display prominent "opt-in" stickers for visitors. Such changes would directly impact the functionality of existing cameras and the value proposition of subscription plans that include these AI perks. While specific indoor models (pre-2018) may still be eligible for payouts from the previous FTC settlement fund, the current lawsuit focuses on the future of biometric features for all Ring users.

## What to do now

![What to do now](/img/ring-facial-recognition-lawsuit-impact-2026/inline-4.webp)


For Ring users and those considering a purchase, understanding how to manage privacy settings and explore alternatives is crucial in light of the ongoing facial recognition lawsuit. Taking proactive steps can help safeguard your biometric data and ensure your security setup aligns with your privacy preferences.

### Step-by-Step Privacy Audit for Ring Users

To manage your privacy settings and opt out of biometric scanning features on your Ring device, follow these steps:

1.  **Open the Ring App:** Launch the Ring application on your smartphone or tablet.
2.  **Navigate to Device Settings:** Select the specific Ring camera you wish to adjust.
3.  **Access "Pro Features":** Look for a section or toggle labeled "Pro Features" or "Advanced Features" within the device settings.
4.  **Disable 'Familiar Faces':** Within "Pro Features," locate the "Familiar Faces" option and toggle it off. This will prevent your camera from scanning and storing face prints of visitors.
5.  **Disable 'Active Warnings':** If present, also consider toggling off "Active Warnings" if you wish to limit automated AI responses based on detected activity.
6.  **Review Shared Access:** Go to the "Control Center" or "Account Settings" within the Ring App and review "Shared Users." Ensure that only trusted individuals have access to your account, as shared users may be able to view and tag faces without your direct knowledge or consent. Remove any users who no longer require access.

### Top 3 Subscription-Free Alternatives with Local AI

For users concerned about cloud-based biometric data processing and recurring subscription costs, several brands offer "edge AI" solutions that perform facial recognition and other smart detections directly on the camera or a local hub, keeping your biometric data off the cloud.

1.  **Eufy Security:** Eufy cameras, particularly models like the eufyCam 3 (S330) and the SoloCam S40, leverage a HomeBase 3 hub for local AI processing. This allows for on-device facial recognition, vehicle detection, and pet detection without a monthly subscription, storing footage and biometric data locally.
2.  **Reolink:** Reolink offers several cameras with local AI capabilities, such as the Reolink Argus 3 Pro and various NVR-based systems. These devices can differentiate between people, vehicles, and pets directly on the camera, reducing [false alerts](https://quvii.com/learn/reolink-camera-motion-detection-false-alerts-fix) and eliminating the need for cloud subscriptions for basic smart detection. Data is stored on a local microSD card or an NVR.
3.  **Arlo (with local storage options):** While Arlo offers cloud subscriptions, many of its cameras, like the Arlo Ultra 2, also support local storage via a SmartHub, allowing some smart detections to occur without continuous cloud reliance. However, advanced AI features often still require an [Arlo Secure](https://quvii.com/blog/arlo-secure-app-6-26-0-firmware-issues) subscription.

This article addresses the pressing concerns surrounding Ring's facial recognition technology in 2026. We'll delve into the specifics of the recent class-action lawsuit, its implications for buyers and existing owners, and concrete steps you can take to protect your privacy.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Is Ring still facing lawsuits over privacy?
Yes, in June 2026, a federal class-action lawsuit, `Sigwalt v. Amazon`, was filed in Seattle, specifically targeting Ring's "Familiar Faces" feature. This lawsuit alleges that Ring cameras scan and store biometric data without consent.

### Can I opt out of Ring's facial recognition?
Yes, you can disable "Familiar Faces" within the Ring App. Navigate to the "Pro Features" section in your device settings and toggle off the "Familiar Faces" option. You should also review "Shared Users" to ensure only trusted individuals have access to your account.

### Is Ring's "Familiar Faces" feature available everywhere in the US?
No, the "Familiar Faces" feature is currently disabled in Illinois, Texas, and Portland, Oregon, due to strict biometric privacy laws in those regions. Availability may also be impacted by the outcome of ongoing legal challenges.

### How long does Ring store biometric data?
Ring officially states it deletes "unrecognized" face data after 30 days and "recognized" data (tagged as "Familiar Faces") after 180 days. However, these claims are currently disputed by the `Sigwalt v. Amazon` lawsuit.

### Do I need a subscription for Ring's facial recognition?
Yes, the "Familiar Faces" feature is a premium AI perk that requires a subscription to the "Ring Home Premium" tier or the "Pro Intelligence" add-on. It is not included with basic Ring Protect plans.
