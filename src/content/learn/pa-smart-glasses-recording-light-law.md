# Pennsylvania's Proposed Smart Glasses Recording Light Law: What You Need to Know

Pennsylvania House Bill 2603, introduced in June 2026, proposes to require smart glasses and other wearable recording devices manufactured, sold, or used in the state to have a visible indicator light when recording audio or video, and would make it illegal to disable or obscure this light.

Pennsylvania House Bill 2603 (HB 2603), introduced by Representative Joe Ciresi in June 2026, represents a significant shift in how the Commonwealth regulates "passive" surveillance technology. The bill seeks to modernize the Pennsylvania Wiretapping and Electronic Surveillance Control Act by specifically addressing the unique privacy challenges posed by wearable cameras.

Under the proposed law, any "wearable recording device" must feature a "visual indicator"—typically a bright LED—that stays illuminated for the entire duration of an active recording. This applies to devices that capture video, audio, or both. Crucially, the bill's jurisdiction is broad: it covers devices manufactured in the state, sold by Pennsylvania retailers, and even those used by individuals within state lines, regardless of where the device was originally purchased.

### Key Provisions of HB 2603

The legislation is designed to ensure that recording is never a "secret" act. The following table summarizes the core mandates within the current draft of the bill:

| Provision | Requirement Details |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Visual Indicator** | Must be a light or physical indicator clearly visible to those in the camera's field of view. |
| **Anti-Tampering** | Illegal to obscure (tape over), disable (via software), or modify the indicator. |
| **Retailer Mandate** | Sellers must provide written notice of PA recording laws at the point of sale. |
| **Manufacturer Duty** | Devices must be designed so the indicator cannot be turned off while the sensor is active. |
| **Legal Framework** | Amends 18 Pa. Cons. Stat. § 5703 (Wiretapping and Surveillance). |

### Scope of "Wearable Recording Device"

While the public discourse often focuses on "smart glasses" like those produced by Meta or Snap, the bill’s language is intentionally broad. It defines a wearable recording device as any electronic device capable of recording or transmitting data (video or audio) that is designed to be worn on the body. This includes:

*   Smart glasses and Augmented Reality (AR) headsets.
*   Body-worn cameras (excluding those used by law enforcement, which are governed by separate statutes).
*   Neck-worn cameras or "vlog" cams.
*   Smartwatches with integrated cameras.

## Why it exists

![Why it exists](/img/pa-smart-glasses-recording-light-law/inline-2.webp)


The primary driver behind HB 2603 is the erosion of "the expectation of privacy" in public and semi-private spaces. Unlike a smartphone, which must be held up to record—signaling intent to those nearby—smart glasses allow for hands-free, eye-level recording that is often indistinguishable from simply looking at someone.

### Addressing Privacy in the Digital Age

Legislators have expressed concern that without a mandated visual cue, wearable devices could be used for clandestine harassment, stalking, or the unauthorized collection of biometric data via real-time facial recognition. According to the Pennsylvania House Co-Sponsorship Memoranda, the bill aims to provide a "common-sense safeguard" that mirrors the physical cues we rely on with traditional photography.

Furthermore, many existing devices on the market already include these lights, but they are often small, dim, or easily bypassed with a small piece of electrical tape. HB 2603 seeks to close these loopholes by making the act of hiding the light a legal violation in itself.

### The Two-Party Consent Context

Pennsylvania is one of a handful of "two-party consent" (or all-party consent) states regarding audio recording. Under 18 Pa. Cons. Stat. § 5704, it is generally illegal to record a private conversation unless everyone involved has given their permission. 

In a world of wearable tech, obtaining verbal consent from every bystander in a park or coffee shop is practically impossible. The proposed "recording light" acts as a functional proxy for notice. While it does not automatically grant the wearer the right to record private conversations, it removes the "secret" element of the recording, which is a central component of wiretapping violations.

## How it works under the hood

![How it works under the hood](/img/pa-smart-glasses-recording-light-law/inline-3.webp)


From a technical perspective, HB 2603 places the burden of compliance on the hardware architecture. It is not enough for a manufacturer to include a light; that light must be "hard-wired" or firmware-locked to the camera's power state.

### Technical Requirements for Manufacturers

If passed, manufacturers would need to ensure that the visual indicator is "non-disablable." In many current-generation smart glasses, the recording LED is controlled by software, which can be modified by third-party apps or "jailbroken" firmware to stay dark while the camera is active. HB 2603 would require Pennsylvania-compliant hardware to have a physical or logic-gate link between the camera sensor and the LED.

### User Responsibilities and Tampering Prevention

For the end-user, the law creates a new category of "prohibited conduct." Even if a device is purchased legally, the user commits a violation if they:
1.  Apply any opaque material (stickers, paint, tape) over the indicator.
2.  Use "hacked" firmware to bypass the light.
3.  Operate the device if the indicator is broken or malfunctioning.

This creates a "strict liability" scenario where the mere act of recording without a functioning light constitutes a breach of the law, regardless of the user's intent.

## Real-world implications

![Real-world implications](/img/pa-smart-glasses-recording-light-law/inline-4.webp)


The impact of HB 2603 extends beyond the people wearing the glasses; it reshapes the retail landscape and the expectations of the general public.

### Impact on Users and Bystanders

For bystanders, the law provides a clear visual signal to "opt-out" of a social interaction or move away if they do not wish to be recorded. It reduces the social friction and "surveillance anxiety" associated with AR technology. 

For users, the law adds a layer of legal risk. A user who forgets to remove a decorative sticker from the frame of their glasses could technically be in violation of state law if that sticker obscures the LED. 

### Manufacturer and Retailer Compliance

The most immediate impact will be felt by Pennsylvania retailers (both big-box stores like Best Buy and small independent electronics shops). Retailers would be required to:
*   Verify that the wearable devices they stock meet the PA visual indicator standards.
*   Provide a written disclosure at the point of sale (digital or paper) explaining Pennsylvania's recording laws.

**Potential Penalties for Non-Compliance:**

| Party | Violation | Penalty |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Retailer** | First Offense | Written Warning |
| **Retailer** | Second Offense | Fine up to $500 |
| **Retailer** | Third+ Offense | Fine up to $1,000 per violation |
| **Manufacturer** | Selling Non-Compliant Units | Potential per-unit civil penalties |
| **User** | Disabling/Obscuring Light | Potential misdemeanor under Wiretap Act |

### Broader Market Effects and Precedent

If Pennsylvania successfully implements HB 2603, it may create a "California Effect," where manufacturers standardize their products to the strictest state's requirements rather than maintaining separate inventories. However, if the law is too restrictive, some manufacturers might simply choose to stop shipping wearable camera products to Pennsylvania addresses, similar to how some "all-party consent" states have seen limited availability of certain call-recording apps.

## Common misconceptions

![Common misconceptions](/img/pa-smart-glasses-recording-light-law/inline-5.webp)


As with any new technology legislation, several myths have surfaced regarding the scope and power of HB 2603.

### Consent vs. Notification
**Misconception:** If my glasses have a recording light on, I am legally allowed to record anyone, anywhere.
**Correction:** The light provides *notification*, not *consent*. In Pennsylvania, you still cannot record private conversations where there is a "justifiable expectation of non-interception" without the consent of all parties. The light helps people know they are being recorded so they can consent or leave, but it does not override existing wiretapping laws.

### State vs. Federal Laws
**Misconception:** This is a new federal law that applies to the whole U.S.
**Correction:** HB 2603 is a state-level bill. While there have been discussions in Congress about "The Wearable Privacy Act," there is currently no federal mandate for recording indicators on consumer wearable devices.

### Scope of Device Coverage
**Misconception:** This law only targets Meta Ray-Bans.
**Correction:** The bill is "device-agnostic." It applies to any wearable capable of recording, including high-end AR headsets, body-worn "action cams" used by hikers, and even smart jewelry with integrated microphones if they are used to record data.

### Current Status of the Bill
**Misconception:** I can be fined today for taping over my camera light.
**Correction:** As of June 2026, HB 2603 is proposed legislation. It must pass through the Pennsylvania House and Senate and be signed by the Governor before it becomes enforceable law. Users should monitor the Pennsylvania General Assembly website for the effective date.

## Decision Framework: Navigating Wearable Privacy

If you are considering a wearable camera in Pennsylvania, use this framework to ensure you remain compliant with both current and proposed regulations.

*   **If you are a Content Creator:** Prioritize devices with high-visibility LEDs (like the Snap Spectacles ring). Always assume that in Pennsylvania, "stealth" recording of audio in private settings is a legal liability, regardless of the light.
*   **If you are a Renter/Homeowner:** For home security, stick to stationary cameras. Wearable cameras used inside your own home are generally safer from a "public privacy" standpoint, but recording guests without notice still triggers Pennsylvania's two-party consent requirements.
*   **If you are a Small Business Owner:** If you provide smart glasses to employees (e.g., for inventory or remote assistance), you must ensure the devices are PA-compliant and that you have updated your employee handbook to reflect the visual indicator mandate.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Does HB 2603 apply to police body cameras?
No. Law enforcement body-worn cameras in Pennsylvania are governed by Act 22 of 2017, which provides specific exemptions and requirements for police recording. HB 2603 is targeted at consumer and commercial wearable devices.

### What happens if the recording light on my glasses breaks?
Under the proposed language of HB 2603, it would be illegal to "use" a wearable recording device if the visual indicator is not functioning. You would need to have the device repaired by the manufacturer to continue using its recording features legally in Pennsylvania.

### Can I still use smart glasses for navigation if I'm not recording?
Yes. The bill specifically targets the "recording or transmission" of data. If the camera and microphone are inactive (e.g., you are only using the glasses for a Heads-Up Display or audio playback), the visual indicator does not need to be illuminated.
